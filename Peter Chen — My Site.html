Origin  ·  新疆
I  —  Origin
"Before I learned to build things, I learned that the world sounds different depending on where inside it you were born."

I grew up in Xinjiang, in the far west of China — a place where different languages, rhythms, and ways of moving through the world share the same streets. I absorbed it before I had categories for it. Multiplicity was not a concept I was taught. It was simply the texture of ordinary life.


This is where the curiosity about language began — not as a school subject, but as something more immediate. English arrived later and felt like another door opening onto a question I was already quietly asking: how many ways of understanding the world are there, and what do they hold in common underneath?


Wuhan came after — for university, for engineering, for a completely different kind of rigor. Load-bearing structures, failure modes, the precise logic of systems. It taught me how to think with discipline. But the instinct toward translation was already formed by then. I arrived in Wuhan already asking questions that blueprints alone couldn't answer.

There is an education available only through displacement — the kind that strips away the assumptions you didn't know you were making.

Melbourne was first. I came for a Masters degree, and stayed because the city rewarded patience — it moved at the pace of a good conversation, layered and unhurried, genuinely interested in ideas for their own sake. I did some of my clearest thinking there.

Sydney followed, and asked different things of me. Sharper, faster, more nakedly ambitious — a city that doesn't let you disappear into reflection for very long. Two years of that friction taught me things that a quieter environment couldn't have. There are cities that show you what you are, and cities that show you what you aren't.


I am back in Melbourne now. Not as someone who went somewhere and retreated, but as someone who completed a circuit and landed differently. The city feels the same. I am the one who changed.


The movement between them mattered as much as either place alone.

Translation
III  —  Translation
"Language is only the outermost layer of what needs translating."

Below it: time, humor, directness, silence — the unspoken grammar of how a society holds itself together. You become a student of these things without deciding to. The curriculum begins the moment you land.

Cultural transition is not a problem to be solved. It is a condition to be inhabited. You don't reach a point where the translation is finished. You simply get better at holding two ways of understanding simultaneously — each one illuminating what the other takes for granted.


Living across cultures doesn't divide your identity. It deepens it. The origin stays present and load-bearing, while the new place adds dimensions it couldn't have anticipated. What you become is not half of either. It is something that didn't exist before you crossed.


I notice it most in meetings — moments when the room is almost communicating but not quite, when the language is shared but the assumptions underneath it aren't. That gap is where I seem to find myself most naturally. Not because I planned it, but because I grew up understanding that the space between perspectives is worth inhabiting.

Sustainability is not an industry. It is a way of reading the world — a commitment to tracing consequences further than is comfortable.

Systems  ·  The Work
IV  —  Systems

How things hold together.

My interest in sustainability didn't arrive through a manifesto. It arrived through attention — the slow observation that energy, cities, policy, and people are not separate conversations. They are the same conversation, conducted in different rooms with different vocabularies and too few people willing to move between them.


What I find most compelling at this intersection is not the technology or the metrics. It is the communication challenge: how do you make the relationship between present choices and future consequences legible to the people who need to act on them? How do you translate urgency without abandoning nuance?

Energy & Climate

The transition is technical and political and social — simultaneously. I am drawn to the intersections, where the real friction lives and where the most durable solutions have to be found.

Cross-Cultural Communication

What gets lost in translation between cultures, disciplines, and scales of thinking. And what it takes — patience, precision, a willingness to stay in the gap — to recover it.

Cities & Infrastructure

The built environment as a record of a society's priorities. Infrastructure as argument — about what we believe the future should look like, and who it is being built for.

Now  ·  Still Becoming
V  —  Now
"The most honest thing I can say is that I am not finished."

I am back in Melbourne — which is where this version of me feels most at home. I think about energy and cities and the systems that connect them. I work across disciplines and cultures because that is where the most interesting problems tend to be: in the gap between two frames of reference that haven't learned to speak to each other yet.


I started in a place where different worlds existed in the same street. I ended up in a country still working out what it is. Both are, in their way, defined by the question of how things fit together across difference — which turns out to be the question I keep returning to, in every context.


I remain curious. I remain grateful for the distance between where I started and where I am — and for everything I don't yet understand about where I'm going.


If something here resonates — I would be glad to hear from you.
